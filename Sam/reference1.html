Reducing social vulnerability can decrease both human suffering and economic loss. CDC Social Vulnerability Index uses 15 U.S. census variables at tract level to help local officials identify communities that may need support in preparing for hazards; or recovering from disaster. The Geospatial Research, Analysis, and Services Program (GRASP) created and maintains CDC’s Social Vulnerability Index. 1 Household HOUSEHOLD Whatever 2 Wealth, Income, and Education WEALTH Whatever 3 Children and Youth CHILDREN Whatever 4 Race, Ethnicity, and Language RACE Whatever 0 - Select one SVI ... 1 Overall SVI SVI "The Social Vulnerability Index uses U.S. Census data to determine the relative social vulnerability of every census tract. The SVI ranks each tract on 15 social factors and groups them into four related themes. Each tract receives a separate ranking for each of the four themes, as well as an overall ranking" (CDC). SVI was originally intended to help public health officials direct assistance to vulnerable communities before, during, and after an emergency disaster. The higher the percentile ranking, the more vulnerable a community is estimated to be overall based on socio-economic status, household composition and disability, minority status and language, and housing and transportation. Conversely, the lower the percentile ranking, the less vulnerable a community is estimated to be overall based on those same categories.
